Don't Consume, Create

The realization that the habit of consuming online content takes time that would be naturally spent creating, had we given it the chance.

August 12, 2025

I have been struggling with the idea of productivity for quite some time, since the start of the PhD studentship actually. In my quest to push back against procrastination, I felt the need to reduce my screen time. I am an avid YouTube user, averaging 4-5 hours of daily YouTube app usage. Definitely too much. For about a year I have kept the screen of my phone in shades of gray i.e., removing all color. The idea was to make it less appealing to look at, and therefore less captivating. This worked for maybe one or two months. Eventually I became used to it, and the habit of opening the YT app every free (or busy) moment came back. Moreover, having a greyscale screen makes you unable to see the colors of the photo you take. As soon as I had a new phone, I ditched the greyscale screen. Back to ground zero.

I changed phone in December last year, now we're in August. I cannot say why, but last week I felt my relationship with the YT app deteriorating. I just started to pay attention to how much time I was spending on it. It suddenly became real, tangible. I found myself questioning "Is this drugs?" while coping for some work-related stress by scrolling YT shorts every other 15 minutes. If that is not drugs, it surely looks like it from the outside. Since when have I been spending all extra time, micro-pauses and procrastination periods by watching YT videos? For the past... ten years? Twelve?

And then it hit me. What would happen if I just stopped? What would I do with all that time? How would it feel?

I am currently challenging myself. No YouTube. Straight up. I won't watch videos to spend idle time at least until the weekend. I will try moving past this weekend into next week without opening the YT app. I started today, and I can already tell that there is some visible difference in how I feel. Initially, the habit kicks in and my thumb just goes through the motion to launch the app. That is ingrained muscle memory, and now that I'm writing it I feel this should not be a thing. When I stop and remind myself that I just want to reappropriate myself of that time YT takes away from me (or rather, the time I systematically put into YT), I stop and put the phone face down. I look straight in front of me, and let the craving wash away.

The first question that comes to mind is "what now?". I feel lost for a minute. Then, naturally, so many alternative ways to spend time pop up as plausible, preferrable answers. If I'm at work, I realize I don't need that scrolly-scrolly pause, I actually have things to do. Thoughts about how to solve my problems don't have to wait for my micro- (or macro-) procrastination now to show up in my head. If I cannot solve my problems now, I do not give myself the short-lived dopamine hit by watching a video. I give myself the luxury of thinking more about that problem.

Afterwork I am usually mentally depleted. No YouTube videos to pass the time before, during, after dinner. What now, then? Today I came back home and I worked out. I then had dinner looking at the view outside the window: it is a beautiful summer day on the northern coast of Brittany, and it deserves contemplation. I kept reading "Networks" by Mark Newman after dinner, I'm almost through Chapter 5. Then I cloned the github page repository onto this old PC, and started blogging.

I feel creative. And I feel that the message of this post is already well delineated. It has been only one day, but I think I have learned something important firsthand: when I free time from consumption, that time is filled with creative or otherwise body- or mind-healing activities. In other words, time snatched from consumption of useless on-line content tends to become time for self-expression. For creation.

Why consume when you could create?
