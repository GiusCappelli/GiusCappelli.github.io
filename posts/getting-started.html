Getting Started

January 15, 2025

Lately, I've been thinking a lot about my work within the my PhD studentship. It has been a process where my perception of the self has vastly changed, and where I have got to truly know my limits in the work as a researcher. Now that I am in the last months, and that I have to tie up all loose ends, I look back and see many of the mistakes I've done and the misconception I have believed in for far too long.

The research work has nothing to do with a conventional job. Not that I've ever done anything remotely close to a conventional job. Research work is ascribed to knowledge work. If I understand correctly, this is work where you mostly use your brain, you rarely get up from your desk, and you are in a constant state process of information gathering and decision making. Knowledge job can be very frustrating if seen through the lense of common concepts like productivity or return on investment.

The reason is rather simple, yet it was very hard for me to grasp. In a knowledge job, as in research, what is valued is not how hard you work, but the quality of your thinking. This reads at first like a eye-rolling-inducing sentence that angers everyone in the room. But the truth is that success in a PhD or research endeavor is synonym with clarity or rather, I think, three stages of clarity. These three stages are:

  1. Clarity on the "problem" i.e., its nature and definition;
  2. Clarity on the body of existing knowledge that surrounds it (the "overview" that has to show up in every paper");
  3. Clarity on the steps required to solve that specific problem.


A normal job requires the production of something and hard, prolongued work can increase the amount of something that is obtained at the end of a day. A knowledge job requires clarity. There are something(s) that are generally mistaken for productivity markers in a knowledge job. For a researcher, for example, the number of papers written in a year, or the number of citations on each publication, are an index of "worth".

These are indeed markers of how good you are as a researcher. The problem i.e., what took me an embarassing time to realize, is that these are also really the collateral effect of having obtained the three clarities I discussed above. When someone has reasoned long enough on a problem, why it exists, what is done to deal with it, and what could be done to tackle it better, the executive part of the research job (experimental work, writing papers, discussing results with peers) becomes a natural extension of the reasoning that preceded it.

This feels oh so obvious and, honestly, too late for what is my situation at the moment. I whish I had understood this sooner. The experience I know have tells me that any work must be backloaded with heaps and heaps of thinking. This stupidly feels like wasted time when deadlines are closing in. But that's just my inexperience. If I want to produce anything with a smidge of quality in it, I have to have thought about it for long, and have produced good grade clarity to sustain it. Otherwise there is no point.
